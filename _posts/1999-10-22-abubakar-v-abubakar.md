## Case Title and Citation

Aminin L. Abubakar, petitioner, vs. Aurora A. Abubakar, respondent.  
G.R. No. 134622, 1999-10-22  
Supreme Court - First Division  
Ponente: DAVIDE, JR., C.J.

---

## Facts

- Aminin L. Abubakar (AMININ) and Aurora A. Abubakar (AURORA) were married in Jolo, Sulu, on 1978-05-01 in accordance with Islamic law.
- Sometime in February 1996, AURORA filed in the 1st Shari'ah Circuit Court of Isabela, Basilan Province a complaint for "Divorce with Prayer for Support and Damages," docketed as Case No. 537, alleging among other things that AMININ contracted a subsequent marriage without her consent in violation of Articles 27 and 162 of Presidential Decree No. 1083 (Code of Muslim Personal Laws).
- The pre-trial order dated 1997-03-21 limited the trial issue to the determination of "the rights or the respective shares of the (parties) with respect to the property subject of partition after divorce."
- The properties identified for partition (hereafter PROPERTIES) were:
  - A half unit of a duplex on Lot No. 1845-B-2, Transfer Certificate of Title No. T-86,898, Tumaga Por Centro, Zamboanga City (the other half owned/occupied by Jackaria M. Mohammad and wife);
  - A 550-square-meter lot adjacent, Lot No. 1845-B-3 under Transfer Certificate of Title No. 86,899; and
  - A house and lot at Block 2 (Lot 44), Kasanyangan Village, Jolo, Sulu, Transfer Certificate of Title No. T-1820 (240 sq. m.).
- On 1997-08-29, the Circuit Court issued an order dissolving the marriage, awarding and distributing the PROPERTIES equally between the parties as co-owners, and ordering AMININ to pay AURORA P10,000 as support for the three-month 'idda.
- AURORA appealed only "as far as it involves the issue of partition of property, and not to the grant of divorce and damages."
- On 1998-05-20, the 3rd Shari'ah Judicial District Court of Zamboanga City (appellate court) issued a decision that, inter alia, affirmed the divorce, distributed the listed properties equally, additionally included an agricultural real estate in Alicia, Zamboanga del Sur as part of common property to be partitioned, ordered AMININ to pay P110,000 as support in arrears (P5,000 monthly for 22 months), and awarded P50,000 as moral damages.
- AMININ filed a motion for reconsideration arguing that the appellate court erred in awarding moral damages, support in arrears, and partition of the Alicia lot because these issues were not raised on appeal; the appellate court denied the motion on 1998-07-15.
- AMININ petitioned the Supreme Court, which rendered judgment on 1999-10-22 reversing the appellate court's decision and reverting to the Circuit Court's 1997-08-29 order.

---

## Issues

1. Did the appellate court have authority to award moral damages although the appellant did not assign error on that matter in the appeal?
2. Did the appellate court have authority to grant support in arrears though the appellant did not raise it on appeal?
3. Did the appellate court have authority to include and order partition of the agricultural lot in Alicia, Zamboanga del Sur when that issue was not raised on appeal?

---

## Ruling

1. No - The appellate court erred in awarding moral damages because the issue was not raised or assigned as error on appeal.
2. No - The appellate court erred in granting support in arrears because the appellant did not seek review of that issue on appeal.
3. No - The appellate court erred in ordering partition of the Alicia lot as the matter was not part of the appeal and thus beyond the scope of review.

---

## Reasoning / Ratio Decidendi

- Pre-trial orders are procedural devices under the Rules of Court (Rules 18 and 118) intended to limit and simplify the issues to be tried; the stipulations in a pre-trial order bind the parties and define the scope of trial.
- AURORA and AMININ agreed, and the pre-trial order specified, that the trial issue would be limited to partition of the identified PROPERTIES; the Circuit Court's 1997-08-29 order was consistent with that pre-trial order.
- Under Rule 51, Section 8 of the 1997 Rules of Civil Procedure, an appellate court will not consider errors not assigned in the appeal unless they affect jurisdiction, are plain or clerical errors, or are closely related to an assigned error. The appellate court's modifications did not concern jurisdiction nor were they clerical or plain errors.
- Precedent: The Court relied on prior decisions and authorities construing the appellate limitation rule, including Development Bank of the Philippines v. Court of Appeals, and Bella v. Court of Appeals, which held that an appellate court lacks power to alter matters not raised on appeal.
- Applying these principles, the Supreme Court concluded the appellate court exceeded its authority by adding the Alicia lot to the partition, increasing support to P110,000, and awarding P50,000 moral damages when these matters were not presented in the appeal.

---

## Doctrine / Legal Principle

- Pre-trial orders bind the parties and limit the issues triable at trial.
- An appellate court may only correct or modify errors that are assigned and argued on appeal, except for jurisdictional issues, plain errors, or clerical mistakes.
- Matters not raised on appeal are final and beyond the appellate court's power to alter.
- Procedural stipulations made by parties in pre-trial cannot be disregarded by appellate review.

---

## Disposition

- The petition is GRANTED.
- The decision of the 3rd Shari'ah Judicial District Court of Zamboanga City dated 1998-05-20 (Appeal Case No. 01-97) and the order dated 1998-07-15 are REVERSED AND SET ASIDE.
- The case is REVERTED to the 1st Shari'ah Circuit Court of Isabela, Basilan Province order dated 1997-08-29 (Case No. 537).
- No pronouncement as to costs.

## Significance / Notes

- Reinforces the binding effect of pre-trial orders and the parties' control over the issues to be litigated.
- Affirms the limited scope of appellate review under Rule 51, Section 8: appellate courts should not decide or grant relief on matters not presented or assigned in the appeal.
- Emphasizes procedural finality and promotes judicial efficiency by preventing appellate courts from reopening issues the appellant chose not to pursue.
- The ruling applies to appeals from Shari'ah courts as well as regular courts where procedural rules and appellate limitations are applicable.
- Practically, parties must properly assign and argue errors on appeal to obtain relief; failure to do so forecloses appellate modification of unassigned matters.
