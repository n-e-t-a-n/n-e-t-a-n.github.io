excerpt: The Supreme Court granted the petition, holding that the transfer of DA-RFU XII to Koronadal City was an executive function beyond judicial inquiry and that the trial court abused its discretion in issuing a preliminary injunction; the Court of Appeals' dismissal of the Rule 65 petition was properly assailed under Rule 45.

## Who were the parties in this case and what are their roles?

<details>
<summary><em>Show Answer</em></summary>
<p>
The petitioner is the Republic of the Philippines, represented in this case by Abusama M. Alid, in his capacity as Officer-in-Charge and Regional Executive Director of the Department of Agriculture - Regional Field Unit XII (DA-RFU XII). The respondents are a group of officials and employees of DA-RFU XII (named individually in the case: Abdulwahab A. Bayao, Osmeña I. Montañer, Rakma B. Buisan, Helen M. Alvarez, Neila P. Limba, Elizabeth B. Pusta, Anna Mae A. Sideno, Udtog B. Tabong, John S. Kamenza, Delia R. Subaldo, Dayang W. Macmod, Florence S. Tayuan), who brought the action in their own behalf and in behalf of other officials and employees of DA-RFU XII. The petitioner represents the executive agency implementing a transfer; respondents are the agency employees opposing that transfer.
</p>
</details>

## What government directive triggered the dispute and what did it provide?

<details>
<summary><em>Show Answer</em></summary>
<p>
The dispute was triggered by Executive Order No. 304, issued on March 30, 2004, which designated Koronadal City as the regional center and seat of the SOCCSKSARGEN Region (Region XII). Section 2 of that Order provided that all departments, bureaus, and offices of the national government in the SOCCSKSARGEN Region shall transfer their regional seat of operations to Koronadal City. This re-designation formed the basis for subsequent directives to implement the transfer of various regional offices, including the DA-RFU XII office.
</p>
</details>

## What specific administrative directive did the Department of Agriculture issue to implement E.O. No. 304?

<details>
<summary><em>Show Answer</em></summary>
<p>
On April 1, 2005, the Department of Agriculture Undersecretary for Operations Edmund J. Sana issued a memorandum directing Officer-in-Charge Abusama M. Alid, Regional Executive Director of DA-RFU XII, to immediately effect the transfer of the administrative, finance, and operations base of RFU XII from Cotabato City to Koronadal City, in compliance with E.O. No. 304. The memorandum allowed interim holding of offices at ATI building in Tantangan and Tupi Seed Farm but specified that the main office shall be within Koronadal City. It required an action plan by 6 April 2005 and commencement of execution by 16 April 2005 so personnel could use the summer break to arrange the transfer.
</p>
</details>

## What actions did the DA-RFU XII employees take to oppose the transfer before litigation?

<details>
<summary><em>Show Answer</em></summary>
<p>
The employees, acting through a memorandum dated April 22, 2005 addressed to DA Secretary Arthur Yap and other communications, opposed the implementation. Their opposition included assertions that former President Gloria Macapagal-Arroyo had publicly pronouced that the regional seat would remain in Cotabato City, that only certain departments were excluded from suspension of E.O. No. 304, and that they had appealed to the Secretary to hold implementation in abeyance. They argued that physical facilities existed in Cotabato City to house staff, that Koronadal lacked adequate buildings and would be more expensive, that transferring would cost the government substantial sums (e.g., dislocation pay of over ₱7,200,000.00 and hauling and construction expenses), and raised structural concerns about the proposed ATI building. Their petition was endorsed and moved through internal channels; they also relied on a Petition and other communications to the President and Secretary.
</p>
</details>

## Describe the immediate administrative steps taken in May 2005 and the employees’ response.

<details>
<summary><em>Show Answer</em></summary>
<p>
On May 17, 2005, OIC Abusama M. Alid held a meeting and ordered that the regional office be transferred to the ATI Building in Tantangan and the Tupi Seed Farm, both in South Cotabato and Koronadal City, with a scheduled carry-out on May 21, 2005. In response, the employees filed a Complaint for Injunction with Prayer for Issuance of Writ of Preliminary Injunction and/or Temporary Restraining Order in the Regional Trial Court (RTC), Branch 14 of Cotabato City, on May 18, 2005, seeking to enjoin the transfer.
</p>
</details>

## What did the Regional Trial Court (RTC) do in October 2006?

<details>
<summary><em>Show Answer</em></summary>
<p>
By Order dated October 9, 2006, the Regional Trial Court, Branch 14 of Cotabato City, granted the respondents' prayer for a writ of preliminary injunction, enjoining the transfer of DA-RFU XII from Cotabato City to Koronadal City. The RTC found sufficient grounds to issue a preliminary injunction based on the pleadings and allegations presented by the respondents regarding potential harm, budgetary and procedural questions, and other factual circumstances.
</p>
</details>

## How did the petitioner attempt to challenge the RTC order and what procedural path did it follow?

<details>
<summary><em>Show Answer</em></summary>
<p>
Petitioner filed a Petition for Certiorari under Rule 65 with the Court of Appeals on December 17, 2006, seeking relief from the RTC's order granting the preliminary injunction. The petition argued, among other things, that the RTC order conflicted with the Supreme Court's prior pronouncement in DENR v. DENR Region 12 Employees and that the trial court had improperly interfered with executive prerogatives.
</p>
</details>

## What action did the Court of Appeals take on the Rule 65 petition?

<details>
<summary><em>Show Answer</em></summary>
<p>
The Court of Appeals dismissed the Rule 65 Petition by Resolution dated March 21, 2007 on the ground that the petitioner failed to first file a Motion for Reconsideration (MR) of the RTC order, a prerequisite to a Rule 65 petition absent applicable exceptions. The Court of Appeals later denied the petitioner's motion for reconsideration of its dismissal in a Resolution dated August 16, 2007.
</p>
</details>

## Why was the dismissal by the Court of Appeals appealed to the Supreme Court, and under what rule?

<details>
<summary><em>Show Answer</em></summary>
<p>
Petitioner elevated the matter to the Supreme Court via a Petition for Review on Certiorari under Rule 45, challenging both the March 21, 2007 Resolution dismissing its Rule 65 petition and the August 16, 2007 Resolution denying reconsideration. Petitioner argued that the CA's dismissal was erroneous — asserting exceptions to the MR prerequisite — and asked the Supreme Court to set aside both the CA resolutions and the underlying RTC injunctive order.
</p>
</details>

## What were the main issues the Supreme Court framed for resolution in this case?

<details>
<summary><em>Show Answer</em></summary>
<p>
The Supreme Court summarized the issues as: (I) whether a Petition under Rule 45 is proper to assail the Court of Appeals' Resolutions dismissing the Rule 65 petition; (II) whether the present case falls within the exceptions to the requirement of filing a Motion for Reconsideration before filing a Rule 65 petition; (III) whether petitioner may raise issues not addressed in the assailed CA Resolutions; and (IV) whether the RTC’s issuance of a preliminary injunction against the transfer violated the separation of powers by intruding upon executive discretion concerning transfers.
</p>
</details>

## How did the Supreme Court treat the procedural question whether Rule 45 was the proper remedy?

<details>
<summary><em>Show Answer</em></summary>
<p>
The Supreme Court held that a petition under Rule 45 was the proper remedy to assail the Court of Appeals' March 21 and August 16, 2007 Resolutions. The Court distinguished Rule 65, an original/independent action, from Rule 45, which functions as a continuation of appellate review of resolved matters. Because the CA Resolutions were final adjudications (dismissing the Rule 65 petition and denying reconsideration), they constituted final resolutions within the meaning of Rule 45 and could therefore be reviewed by the Supreme Court under Rule 45. The Court further observed that, absent such review, those CA Resolutions would attain finality and thereby render the RTC order unassailable.
</p>
</details>

## What is the general rule about filing a Motion for Reconsideration before bringing a Rule 65 petition, and why does it exist?

<details>
<summary><em>Show Answer</em></summary>
<p>
The general rule is that the filing of a Motion for Reconsideration with the lower court is a condition sine qua non before a petitioner may seek relief by way of a petition for certiorari under Rule 65. This procedural requirement affords the lower court an opportunity to correct its own errors by re-examining the legal and factual circumstances of the case, thereby promoting finality and judicial economy. The motion serves as a last chance for the trial judge to resolve perceived errors without immediate recourse to extraordinary relief.
</p>
</details>

## What exceptions to the Motion for Reconsideration requirement did the Supreme Court acknowledge?

<details>
<summary><em>Show Answer</em></summary>
<p>
The Supreme Court reiterated well-defined exceptions that obviate the need to file a Motion for Reconsideration before instituting a certiorari petition. These exceptions include: (a) where the order is a patent nullity (e.g., lack of jurisdiction); (b) where the questions raised in the certiorari proceedings have been duly raised and passed upon by the lower court; (c) where there is urgent necessity and further delay would prejudice the government or petitioner, or the subject matter is perishable; (d) where a motion for reconsideration would be useless under the circumstances; (e) where the petitioner was deprived of due process and urgent relief is needed; (f) in criminal cases where relief from an order of arrest is urgent and relief from the trial court is unlikely; (g) where lower court proceedings are nullities for lack of due process; (h) where proceedings were ex parte or petitioner had no opportunity to object; and (i) where the issue is purely one of law or public interest is involved. The Court applied these established exceptions as the test for dispensing with the MR requirement.
</p>
</details>

## Which exception did the Supreme Court find applicable in this case and why?

<details>
<summary><em>Show Answer</em></summary>
<p>
The Supreme Court found the second exception applicable: the questions raised in the certiorari proceedings had already been duly raised and passed upon by the lower court. The petitioner had previously presented the same issues to the RTC—through its motion to dismiss, memorandum, and manifestations and replies—so the RTC had the opportunity to consider those arguments before issuing the injunction. The Court relied on precedent (e.g., Siok Ping Tang v. Subic Bay Distribution) which justified bypassing the MR requirement where the CA was presented with the same arguments already considered by the trial court.
</p>
</details>

## Did the Supreme Court consider any broader public-interest rationale for relaxing procedural requirements in this case?

<details>
<summary><em>Show Answer</em></summary>
<p>
Yes. The Supreme Court observed that procedural rules may be relaxed in the face of compelling public interest concerns, such as the need for stability in public service and the significant implications on the administration of the executive department. The Court noted that the instant case implicated the effective administration of the executive branch—namely, the designation and transfer of a regional center pursuant to an executive directive—and therefore could warrant relaxation of procedural rules if necessary.
</p>
</details>

## How did the Supreme Court address the contention that petitioner raised issues not covered by the CA's Resolutions?

<details>
<summary><em>Show Answer</em></summary>
<p>
The Supreme Court acknowledged that the Court of Appeals' decisions focused primarily on the procedural aspect—whether the petitioner should have filed a Motion for Reconsideration—but, because the petition overcame procedural obstacles, the Supreme Court proceeded to address the substantive issues raised by petitioner. In other words, having resolved that Rule 45 was the proper remedy and that the MR exception applied, the Court found it appropriate to examine the merits, including whether the RTC had exceeded its proper judicial role in enjoining an executive transfer.
</p>
</details>

## What central substantive legal principle did the Supreme Court apply in resolving whether the RTC could enjoin the transfer?

<details>
<summary><em>Show Answer</em></summary>
<p>
The Court applied the principle of separation of powers, emphasizing that the decision to transfer regional offices and to designate regional centers is an executive function intimately related to the President’s power of general supervision over local governments and the executive’s discretion in organizing administrative regions. Consequently, questions pertaining to the wisdom, expediency, or convenience of such transfers fall within the exclusive competence of the executive and are generally beyond judicial inquiry. The Court relied on prior Supreme Court decisions, particularly DENR v. DENR Region 12 Employees, to affirm that the judiciary may not substitute its judgment for the executive's on matters of administrative reorganization and transfer.
</p>
</details>

## How did the Supreme Court interpret DENR v. DENR Region 12 Employees in applying it to this case?

<details>
<summary><em>Show Answer</em></summary>
<p>
The Supreme Court treated DENR v. DENR Region 12 Employees as controlling precedent for the proposition that the transfer of regional offices is an executive decision and that the judiciary cannot inquire into or enjoin such transfers based on arguments about the wisdom or advisability of the move. The Court emphasized that many of the respondents’ concerns—absence of suitable buildings in Koronadal, timing relative to Ramadan, children’s schooling, and consultations—were matters addressing the wisdom or expediency of the transfer and not its legality. DENR thus supported the principle that the judiciary must refrain from intervening in executive organizational choices absent legal infirmity.
</p>
</details>

## What constitutional and statutory authorities did the Supreme Court note as underpinning the executive power to determine regional centers?

<details>
<summary><em>Show Answer</em></summary>
<p>
The Supreme Court noted that the President's power to exercise general supervision over local government units is grounded in the Constitution (Article X, Section 4) and is further reflected in the Local Government Code of 1991 (Republic Act No. 7160), specifically Section 25, which vests the President with supervisory authority over local governments to ensure acts remain within prescribed powers. The Court also cited prior case law (e.g., Abbas v. COMELEC and Chiongbian v. Orbos) recognizing the President's authority to reorganize administrative regions and to determine a regional center as part of that supervisory and reorganization power.
</p>
</details>

## How did the Supreme Court view the respondents’ factual contentions about hardship, lack of buildings, and costs?

<details>
<summary><em>Show Answer</em></summary>
<p>
The Supreme Court characterized those contentions as concerns pertaining to the wisdom and expediency of the transfer rather than its legality. The Court stressed that the judiciary could not resolve disputes grounded in the alleged hardship to employees (e.g., schooling of children, new housing difficulties), the absence of adequate buildings in Koronadal, or disputations over the amount of funds required. Those were matters for the executive to weigh; hence, the trial court erred in issuing an injunction grounded on such considerations. The Court noted that these factors had been previously addressed in DENR and found insufficient to justify judicial interference.
</p>
</details>

## What did the Supreme Court say about the alleged verbal suspension of E.O. No. 304 by the President?

<details>
<summary><em>Show Answer</em></summary>
<p>
The Supreme Court stated that a verbal pronouncement by the President claiming to suspend E.O. No. 304 should not be given weight, emphasizing that executive orders are amended, modified, or revoked only by subsequent orders and must comply with the ordinance power and administrative procedures. The Court reiterated that an executive order is valid unless contrary to law or the Constitution, and that formal action is required to alter an existing executive order — a mere verbal statement lacks the force to suspend E.O. No. 304.
</p>
</details>

## Why did the Supreme Court conclude that the RTC committed grave abuse of discretion?

<details>
<summary><em>Show Answer</em></summary>
<p>
The Supreme Court concluded the RTC committed grave abuse of discretion because it enjoined the transfer — an act within the executive’s domain — based on considerations of expediency and the purported hardships of employees. In doing so, the RTC effectively substituted its judgment for that of the executive branch regarding the wisdom and timing of a policy-driven administrative transfer. Since the ICT of transferring a regional center is an executive function protected by separation of powers, the Court found that the RTC exceeded its authority and thus abused its discretion in issuing the preliminary injunction.
</p>
</details>

## How did the Supreme Court dispose of the case?

<details>
<summary><em>Show Answer</em></summary>
<p>
The Supreme Court granted the petition. It reversed and set aside (1) the Court of Appeals Resolutions dated March 21, 2007 and August 16, 2007 in CA-G.R. SP No. 01457-MIN, and (2) the Regional Trial Court Decision/Order dated October 9, 2006 of Branch 14 of Cotabato City that had granted the writ of preliminary injunction. Effectively, the Supreme Court removed the judicial restraint on the executive's implementation of E.O. No. 304 as it related to DA-RFU XII.
</p>
</details>

## What was the Court's view regarding the remedy sought by the respondents (injunction) in relation to the separation of powers doctrine?

<details>
<summary><em>Show Answer</em></summary>
<p>
The Court viewed the respondents’ use of injunction as an improper indirect attempt to block or nullify an exercise of executive authority. Because the relief sought — preventing the transfer of the regional office — implicated the executive's prerogative to reorganize and determine regional centers, allowing an injunction would have required the judiciary to pass judgment on the wisdom of executive action. The Court reiterated that the judiciary must not direct or restrain legitimate executive functions; thus, the injunction remedy was inappropriate where the issue was one of executive discretion rather than legal illegality.
</p>
</details>

## Did the Supreme Court analyze whether the RTC's factual findings regarding budget and funding were correct?

<details>
<summary><em>Show Answer</em></summary>
<p>
The Supreme Court did address the petitioner's arguments that alleged budgetary calculations and claims of illegal realignment were either exaggerated or within executive discretion and that funds could be sourced from existing administrative funds (e.g., discretionary administrative fund of the Office of the Secretary). However, the Court did not conduct an exhaustive factual re-evaluation of the RTC's budgetary findings because the central error identified was the RTC's encroachment on executive discretion. The Court's focus remained on the nature of the issue—executive wisdom versus legal infirmity—and the inappropriateness of judicial injunction against an executive transfer.
</p>
</details>

## What precedent cases did the Supreme Court cite to support its conclusion on executive prerogative?

<details>
<summary><em>Show Answer</em></summary>
<p>
The Supreme Court cited DENR v. DENR Region 12 Employees as controlling precedent that the judiciary cannot entertain injunctions to force regional offices to remain in a prior location when the transfer is an executive decision. The Court also referenced Abbas v. COMELEC and Chiongbian v. Orbos as authority for the President's power to reorganize administrative regions and determine the regional center. Additionally, the Court relied on general separation of powers principles drawn from cases such as Santiago v. Guingona and classic statements in earlier jurisprudence that the judiciary must not direct or restrain executive or legislative actions from exercising discretion.
</p>
</details>

## How did the Court reconcile the employees' claims of potential irreparable injury with its ultimate ruling?

<details>
<summary><em>Show Answer</em></summary>
<p>
The Court acknowledged the employees' claims of potential hardship, such as schooling disruptions and financial strain, but treated these claims as elements that bear on the wisdom or fairness of the transfer rather than on the legality of the administrative act. The Court observed that such concerns were considered in the DENR precedent and deemed insufficient to justify judicial intervention. The petitioner's counter-arguments suggested that employees would still be paid, could arrange housing, and that allowances could be provided subsequently; but the Court's decisive point was that such factual or policy considerations are for the executive to weigh, and the judiciary should not substitute its own judgment by issuing an injunction.
</p>
</details>

## What did the Supreme Court say about the legal effect of an executive order and how it may be altered?

<details>
<summary><em>Show Answer</em></summary>
<p>
The Supreme Court emphasized that executive orders have legal effect insofar as they are not contrary to law or the Constitution, and that they are altered only by subsequent orders. The Court cited the principle reflected in law that administrative or executive acts, orders and regulations must not be contrary to statutes or the Constitution and that changes to such orders require formal subsequent action. Thus, a verbal statement purporting to suspend an executive order was insufficient; formal executive action is necessary to amend, revoke, or suspend an executive order.
</p>
</details>

## What role did the Court of Appeals’ characterization of the petitioner’s failure to file a Motion for Reconsideration play in the Supreme Court’s analysis?

<details>
<summary><em>Show Answer</em></summary>
<p>
The Court of Appeals dismissed the Rule 65 petition primarily because petitioner did not first file a Motion for Reconsideration with the RTC. The Supreme Court analyzed whether bypassing the MR requirement was justified. Finding that the issues raised in the Rule 65 petition had already been presented to and passed upon by the lower court, and given the public-interest implications, the Supreme Court concluded that the MR prerequisite need not have been strictly applied. Therefore, the CA's dismissal on procedural grounds was overturned, and the Supreme Court proceeded to examine the substantive question of separation of powers.
</p>
</details>

## How did the Supreme Court treat the notion that the CA Resolutions were not "final" and therefore not appealable?

<details>
<summary><em>Show Answer</em></summary>
<p>
The Supreme Court rejected the respondents' contention that the CA Resolutions were not final and thus not subject to Rule 45 review. The Court explained that the CA's dismissal of a Rule 65 petition and its denial of reconsideration are final resolutions for purposes of appellate review. The Court also emphasized the practical effect: if such resolutions were not reviewable, they would attain finality and the underlying RTC order would become effectively unassailable. Hence, Rule 45 provided a proper vehicle to seek review of those CA Resolutions.
</p>
</details>

## What broader administrative principle about reorganization and designation of regional centers can be distilled from the Court’s decision?

<details>
<summary><em>Show Answer</em></summary>
<p>
The broader administrative principle is that decisions about the organization of the executive branch—such as the designation of regional centers and the transfer of regional offices—fall within the executive’s policymaking and supervisory domain and are generally nonjusticiable in the sense that courts should not inquire into or restrain them on grounds of expediency or wisdom. Judicial review is reserved for legality and constitutional conformity, but not for substituting judicial evaluation for executive policy choices. This principle promotes respect for separation of powers and the autonomy of the executive in managing its internal affairs.
</p>
</details>

## Would this decision preclude all judicial review of executive reorganizations? How did the Court clarify the limit of its holding?

<details>
<summary><em>Show Answer</em></summary>
<p>
The decision does not preclude all judicial review of executive reorganizations. The Supreme Court confined its ruling to the proposition that courts will not entertain injunctions aimed at preventing an executive's transfer of regional offices when the dispute is over the wisdom or expediency of the transfer. Judicial review remains available when an executive act is illegal, a patent nullity, or otherwise contrary to law or the Constitution. The Court reiterated the exceptions to the MR requirement (which include patent nullity or lack of jurisdiction) and affirmed that where legal grounds exist (as distinct from mere policy disagreements), courts retain the power to adjudicate.
</p>
</details>

## In light of this case, what are the limits of the remedy of injunction when sought against government administrative actions?

<details>
<summary><em>Show Answer</em></summary>
<p>
The remedy of injunction cannot be used as a tool to second-guess executive decisions that are political, administrative or policy-driven in nature—particularly those addressing organizational arrangements like regional seat designation—where the dispute centers on wisdom, convenience, or operational expediency. Injunctive relief remains available where there is a clear legal infirmity, lack of jurisdiction, denial of due process, or violation of constitutional or statutory mandates. But using injunctions to halt the ordinary exercise of executive discretion in reorganizing government services risks an improper encroachment by the judiciary into executive turf.
</p>
</details>

## How did the Court treat the respondents’ arguments about alleged procedural defects in the issuance of directives for transfer (e.g., lack of appropriation, realignment of funds)?

<details>
<summary><em>Show Answer</em></summary>
<p>
The Court noted the respondents’ claims regarding alleged illegal realignment of funds, authorization issues in memoranda, and lack of appropriation for the transfer. However, it characterized these as executive matters and emphasized that they did not demonstrate a legal nullity or jurisdictional defect that would justify judicial intervention. The Court accepted the petitioner's contention that funds could be sourced from appropriate administrative funds and that the alleged computation of required amounts might have been exaggerated. Overall, the Court found the arguments insufficient to sustain an injunction because they addressed administrative choices rather than clear legal violations.
</p>
</details>

## What can we learn from the Court’s treatment of “verbal pronouncements” by the President in relation to formal administrative acts?

<details>
<summary><em>Show Answer</em></summary>
<p>
The Court’s treatment highlights that informal or verbal statements by a President cannot substitute for formal administrative action when it comes to the amendment, suspension, or revocation of an executive order. The Court reiterated that executive orders are valid until formally changed by subsequent orders and emphasized the need for formal, documented administrative processes for altering executive directives. Thus, a claimed verbal suspension does not create a legally binding suspension of an executive order and should not be relied upon to defeat formal, documented directives.
</p>
</details>

## How did the Supreme Court balance respect for employees’ concerns against principles of public administration?

<details>
<summary><em>Show Answer</em></summary>
<p>
While the Court recognized the genuine nature of employees’ concerns—such as displacement, schooling, and possible economic hardship—it balanced those against the institutional need for the executive to exercise its administrative prerogatives. The Court emphasized that resolving those concerns involves policy trade-offs and logistic planning appropriate to the executive branch. Judicial relief in the form of an injunction was deemed inappropriate because it would amount to substituting judicial policy choices over executive administrative judgments. The Court implied that accommodating employees’ concerns is for the executive to address administratively, not for the judiciary to remedy through injunctive relief unless legal violations are shown.
</p>
</details>

## Summarize the ratio decidendi of the Supreme Court in this case.

<details>
<summary><em>Show Answer</em></summary>
<p>
The ratio decidendi is that the transfer of a regional office and the designation of a regional center pursuant to executive orders are executive acts that implicate the President’s constitutional and statutory supervisory powers over local governments. Therefore, disputes that challenge such transfers on grounds of wisdom, convenience, or policy are non-justiciable; courts must not enjoin these executive acts by substituting their judgment for the executive's. Because the RTC issued a preliminary injunction predicated on considerations of expediency and hardship—matters for executive determination—it acted with grave abuse of discretion. Consequently, the RTC order and the Court of Appeals' dismissal of petitioner’s Rule 65 petition (on procedural grounds) were set aside.
</p>
</details>

## What final relief did the Supreme Court afford and what is its practical effect?

<details>
<summary><em>Show Answer</em></summary>
<p>
The Supreme Court granted the petition, reversing the CA Resolutions and the RTC Order that had enjoined the transfer. Practically, this removed the judicial impediment to the Department of Agriculture's implementation of E.O. No. 304 with respect to DA-RFU XII, allowing the executive branch to proceed with transferring the regional seat to Koronadal City and to manage the logistical, funding, and personnel matters associated with that transfer without a court-ordered injunction obstructing those efforts.
</p>
</details>

## Based on the case, what procedural lesson follows about the interplay of Motion for Reconsideration, Rule 65, and Rule 45 petitions?

<details>
<summary><em>Show Answer</em></summary>
<p>
The procedural lesson is that a Motion for Reconsideration before the trial court is ordinarily a necessary prerequisite before filing a Rule 65 petition, as it allows the lower court to correct its own errors. However, recognized exceptions exist—most pertinently, when the issues raised in the Rule 65 petition have already been presented to and decided by the lower court. In such cases, the Court of Appeals’ dismissal of a Rule 65 petition for failure to file an MR can be reviewed by the Supreme Court via Rule 45, because CA resolutions dismissing Rule 65 petitions are final and thus appealable. The case underscores both the importance of observing procedural prerequisites and the availability of exceptions in the interest of justice and public administration.
</p>
</details>

## Pose and answer a question about how this case might guide a trial court faced with an injunction request challenging an executive administrative action.

<details>
<summary><em>Show Answer</em></summary>
<p>
Question: If a trial court is presented with a request for preliminary injunction to prevent an executive administrative action such as a transfer of a regional office, how should the trial court proceed in light of this decision?

Answer: The trial court should carefully assess whether the challenged matter involves legal illegality, jurisdictional defect, or constitutional violation rather than being a dispute about the wisdom, convenience, or policy of the executive action. If the complaint concerns the latter—policy judgment on reorganization or transfer—the trial court should be cautious about granting injunctive relief because such relief would intrude upon executive prerogatives and risk committing grave abuse of discretion. The trial court should require the plaintiff to demonstrate clear legal grounds for the injunction (e.g., a patent nullity, lack of authority, denial of due process) and should decline to intervene when the controversy centers on administrative discretion. This approach aligns with the Supreme Court’s reaffirmation that the judiciary must not substitute its judgment for that of the executive in matters of administrative organization unless legal infirmity is established.
</p>
</details>
